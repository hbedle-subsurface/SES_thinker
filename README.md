## The short version

Six scenarios. Five thinking styles. Zero energy knowledge required.

This quiz doesn't ask students what they know about energy or what career they want. It puts them in situations they've already been in — a friend group argument, a campus policy that backfired, a feed full of hot takes — and asks what they notice first. The result names the thinking style they just demonstrated, gives it a systems thinking term they've never heard before, and shows them the one OU course that formalizes it.

The energy connection appears only in the result card. By then the student is already reading.

---

## The design logic

"Systems thinking" is jargon. It means nothing to a 19-year-old who hasn't encountered it. But the actual skill — understanding how one thing affects everything else — is something people either naturally do or they don't. And the students who do it have usually been doing it for years without a name for it.

This quiz works by going in the other direction from most recruiting materials. Instead of explaining what systems thinking is and asking students if they do it, it shows students a scenario and lets them answer instinctively. The result then tells them: that instinct you just acted on has a name, people study it, and there's a course at OU built around exactly that way of thinking.

The moment where a student reads "that's the systems term for what you do naturally" and sees their thinking style named in academic language for the first time is the conversion moment. It validates something they already are rather than proposing something they should become.

## The five thinking styles

- **The Cause Tracer** — follows problems backward to their root; won't accept a solution built on an unexamined assumption; systems term: Causal Loop Thinking

- **The Ripple Spotter** — sees second and third-order effects before anyone else in the room; maps what happens after the plan works; systems term: Second-Order Effects Thinking

- **The Stakeholder Scanner** — instinctively notices who's in the room and who isn't; tracks whose interests are being presented as universal when they're actually specific; systems term: Stakeholder Analysis

- **The Time Zoomer** — switches naturally between short-term and long-term frames; recognizes when two people arguing about the same thing are actually arguing about different time horizons; systems term: Temporal Dynamics

- **The Feedback Finder** — sees when a solution is going to create the next version of the same problem; spots the loop before it closes; systems term: Feedback Loop Analysis

Each result includes a direct description of what that thinking style looks like in practice, a strip that names the academic term for it, a full course callout for SES 2123 written specifically for that thinking style, the certificate overview with scholarship information, and two neighboring thinking styles.

**SES 2123: Energy in Society: A Systems Perspective**  
Gen Ed III-SS (Social Science). Taught by Robert Lifset.  
Tues/Thurs 12:00–1:15pm, SEC P0207.

This course explores energy production and consumption as part of a complex system shaped by history, economics, philosophy, politics, society, and culture. Every result card describes this course differently — each description is written for the thinking style that result just identified. The Cause Tracer reads that SES 2123 traces cause-and-effect chains through history. The Feedback Finder reads that it covers how policy has responded to problems in ways that created new ones. Same course, five true descriptions, each one written to land for a specific kind of reader.

## What makes this quiz different from the others

This is the fourth quiz in the SES family. It has the most specific academic target — a single course — and the most indirect approach to getting there.

| Quiz | Hook | Academic target | Best for |
|------|------|-----------------|----------|
| **What's Your Energy?** | Which energy source matches your personality? | SES Certificate broadly | Anyone; pure curiosity hook |
| **What Kind of Energy Person Are You?** | Where do you fit in the energy field? | SES Certificate, career paths | Students already curious about SES |
| **Which Problem Are You Here to Solve?** | Which human problem are you already solving? | SES Certificate, humanities/SS focus | Non-STEM students who wouldn't click energy content |
| **What Kind of Thinker Are You?** | How does your brain work when things get complicated? | SES 2123 specifically | Any student who has ever thought "there should be a word for how I think" |

The thinker quiz is the most transferable of the four — it doesn't require any energy framing to recruit with. A QR code on a poster that says "What kind of thinker are you?" will get clicks from students who would walk past anything that mentions energy, sustainability, or certificates. The connection to SES is the payoff, not the hook.

## Question design

Every question is a scenario the student has already lived. No question assumes knowledge of energy, policy, or academic concepts. The scenarios are:

1. A friend group argument — immediate and personal
2. A gas price spike on their feed — recent and visceral
3. What role they play in complicated group conversations — pure self-recognition
4. A university policy that backfired — on-campus and personal
5. What makes them want to jump into a conversation — pure instinct
6. A company making a big environmental announcement — current events, no prior knowledge needed

The question is always some version of "what do you notice first" or "what's your gut reaction." Never "what would you do" — that question rewards people who know the right answer rather than people who are being honest.

## The result card structure

Each result card has five sections, in deliberate order:

1. **Hero** — the thinking style name and tagline; should land as immediate recognition
2. **The systems term** — the academic name for what they just demonstrated; this is the "oh there's a word for this" moment
3. **What this looks like** — a description of the thinking style in plain language, written to feel like someone who knows them well describing them accurately
4. **The course that formalizes it** — SES 2123 with a description written for their specific thinking style, Gen Ed credit, no-prerequisite callout, instructor name
5. **The certificate** — 15 credits, scholarship, contact info, website
6. **Thinkers like you** — two neighboring styles with a note on how they relate

The email CTA ("Want to know which electives fit your major?") is placed after the card, framed as a single specific question Nova can answer — not a general inquiry about the program.

## Who it's for

Any OU undergraduate, but especially:

- Students who are intellectually curious but haven't found a field that feels like theirs yet
- Students in majors that involve analysis, argumentation, or problem-solving — economics, political science, philosophy, journalism, history, psychology, sociology, pre-law
- Honors College students looking for interdisciplinary academic challenge
- Students who have mentioned "systems thinking" or "big picture thinking" as something they do without knowing it's a real academic concept

Deployment contexts where this quiz works particularly well:

- Posters with no energy language — just "What kind of thinker are you?" and a QR code
- Honors College communications
- Advising sessions for undeclared students
- Intro courses in political science, philosophy, economics, and sociology where systems-level thinking is implicitly taught but rarely named
- Any context where the audience would reject an energy pitch but respond to a self-knowledge pitch
